Analyzing UNESCO's Accessibility Narratives

With this work we will be analyzing a selection of UNESCO documents to understand how their focus on accessibility in heritage has led to a particular hierarchy of word use from 2020 to 2023.

The goal is to identify focuses in specific aspects of accessibility

The project will take into account different types of accessibility: physical, intellectual, economic, social, and cultural and different types of heritage (tangible and intangible, cultural, natural, and digital).

Go directly to the Github Repository

Our Methodology

  • Collect a corpus of UNESCO documents related to heritage and accessibility.

  • Preprocess the text data by removing stop words, stemming or lemmatizing, and tokenizing the documents.

  • Perform topic modeling on the corpus using a tool such as Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) to identify the key topics and themes in the documents. Frequencies and co-occurrence etc.

  • Analyze the topics and themes to identify the specific areas of accessibility and heritage that are being focused on in the documents.

  • Conduct a longitudinal analysis to track changes in focus over time by dividing the corpus into different time periods and analyzing the topics and themes in each period.

  • Use visualization techniques such as word clouds and network graphs to present the findings in an accessible and informative manner.

Corpus selection

Our source of reference for the selection of the documentation has been uniquely the Unesco Digital Library .

We have been performing a specific research on the Digital Library based on the following criteria:

  • Main Topic: (All Words) “Accessibility”
  • Year of publication: from 2020 to 2023
  • Source: Unesco
  • Official language: English

Our search (performed in June 2023) returned a list of 74 documents as result, then in September it arrived to 77.

There are different kinds of documents in the list, most of them classified as programme and meeting documents are messages from the Director-General of Unesco, whitepaper, information papers, report and draft report on specific activities,then we have books, periodical issues and articles.

Our Research Questions

  • How has UNESCO's focus on accessibility in heritage changed over time, and what are the specific areas of accessibility and heritage that they tend to focus on?

  • What types of accessibility (physical, intellectual, economic, social, cultural) are focused on in UNESCO documents?

  • What types of heritage (tangible and intangible, cultural, natural, and digital) are focused on in UNESCO documents?

  • How does the focus of UNESCO’s accessibility efforts vary across different types of heritage (tangible/intangible, cultural/natural/digital)?

The LDA model

We have been going through the LDA visualization method to conduct our analysis.

We describe Latent Dirichlet allocation (LDA), a generative probabilistic model for collections of discrete data such as text corpora. LDA is a three-level hierarchical Bayesian model, in which each item of a collection is modeled as a finite mixture over an underlying set of topics. Each topic is, in turn, modeled as an infinite mixture over an underlying set of topic probabilities. In the context of text modeling, the topic probabilities provide an explicit representation of a document. Journal of Machine Learning Research 3 (2003) 993-1022

Our Conclusions

Our primary objective in this results section is to shed light on the inner workings of the LDA (Latent Dirichlet Allocation) model, its output, and the insights it provides. While the topics generated did not align with our original research questions, this analysis will offer a dive into the model's mechanics and the revelations it offers, hopefully giving some insights on how to enhance and refine it to get closer to our initial question.

Our methodology doesn't afford us to discern or to conclude about the semantic shift in the UNESCO documents regarding Accessibility before and after Covid. However, we might say that there's a clear focus on information accessibility in the UNESCO documents. Information accessibility, according to UNESCO(2023), encompasses a wide range of concerns related to the presence, ease of access, and cost-effectiveness of information. These concerns include aspects such as multilingualism, metadata, interoperability, open-source software, open content, Creative Commons licenses, and the consideration of the unique requirements of individuals with disabilities. We might say that among different types of accessibility, information accessibility is key to UNESCO's ambition towards equitable access to cultural heritage. This might be explained by the rising digitalization of cultural heritage in the world, with digital literacy the major determinant that decides an individual's educational and creative opportunities in this day and age.
