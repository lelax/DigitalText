Understanding LDA Topic Modeling Output

One of the most used visualization tools for LDA is the PyLDAvis. This interactive tool transforms the abstract output of the model into a visual representation that is both intuitive and powerful.
PyLDAvis facilitates a concrete view of our LDA model, providing a comprehensive overview of how topics are distributed, which terms are most important within each topic, and even to some extend how topics relate to one another. By navigating the pyLDAvis chart, we have been able to get a better understanding of the individual topics and as the whole. Here below is an examplary image of the pyLDAvis tool of our model. As you can see it’s been separated into two parts: a graph and the ‘Topic-Term Relevance Plot’. Before diving into both in more detail, a brief overview of what they represent. The graph on the left provides a bird’s-eye view of the LDA model. The circles presented are the 29 topics the model found in the corpus. As you can see there is one circle highlighted in red, this selected topic is being shown in the plot on the right. It provides an overview of the most relevant terms in that topic.

Distribution of Circles

In the pyLDAvis visualization, the distribution of the circles int the graph are important to understand and can give valuable insights. The distribution of circles on the pyLDAvis chart serves as a visual representation of the topics identified by our LDA model. Each circle represents a topic, and its position is not arbitrary; it is determined by two critical factors: relevance and prevalence.

  1. Relevance: The closer a circle is to the center of the visualization, the more relevant it is in the context of our corpus. In other words, topics situated near the center are more prominent across our dataset.
  2. Prevalence: Conversely, topics closer to the edges of the chart are less prevalent in our corpus. Their position reflects their lower occurrence in the overall analysis.

By understanding the spatial distribution of these circles, we gain insights into which topics are central and which are more peripheral in our LDA model.

Not only does the spatial arrangement say something about the prominence of the topic. The relative distance between circles in the pyLDAvis chart also give crucial understanding about topic relationships within our corpus. When circles are closely positioned, it signifies that these topics share common themes and exhibit a high degree of similarity in their content. They often co-occur within the same documents, indicating a strong thematic connection. Conversely, when circles are distantly separated, it implies that the corresponding topics are distinct and dissimilar.

Circle Size (Topic Size)

In pyLDAvis, the size of each circle conveys crucial information about topic importance: Circle size in the pyLDAvis chart corresponds to the prevalence of each topic within our corpus. Topics with larger circles are more prevalent, indicating that they play a more significant role in our analysis. This size-based representation offers a quick and intuitive means of identifying the topics that dominate our corpus.

Topic-Term Relevance Plot

The Topic-Term Relevance Plot is important for better understanding of the individual topics. Each bar represents a term, the height of the bar indicates the term’s relevance to the selected topic. There are 3 parameters/representations that need to be explained:

  1. Term Frequency (TF): This shows how many times a specific term appears within a topic. As you can see each bar has a red and a blue part. The red part represents the Term Frequency, the blue bar the overall frequency of that term.
  2. Term Relevance (TR): This represents the uniqueness of a term within a topic compared to the whole corpus.
  3. Lambda (λ): Lambda is a parameter that controls the balance between these two. By adjusting the lambda slider (on top of the plot) you can retrieve the more unique terms of the topic. When λ is set to 1, the plot shows terms based solely on their frequency within the topic (TF). As λ decreases, it gives more weight to the uniqueness of terms within the topic (TR).
