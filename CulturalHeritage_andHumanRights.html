Cultural Rights are Human Rights

Katja S. Ziegler

Introduction: Links between Cultural Heritage and Human Rights Several links can be established between cultural heritage and human rights:

  1. Cultural rights are human rights. This connection is made in Art. 13 UNCh: - Cultural rights are part of the body of human rights law. - Human rights may be violated through the destruction of ‘culture’, for example if cultural property or cultural heritage is destroyed with ulterior motives, such as to eradicate a group identity. Such acts against cultural property or heritage may, for example, reveal the intent necessary to establish a crime against humanity or genocide.
  2. The protection of cultural rights may indirectly protect human rights more widely and more generally. - Protecting cultural property and cultural heritage may also concomitantly protect human beings as it presumes the observance of certain values. Who destroys objects or expressions of ‘culture’ will be more likely to violate human rights. - Cultural property/heritage and cultural rights both aim – at least to some extent – at protecting human identity. - Cultural identity in its individual dimension of human identity is an aspect of human dignity. Thus, the protection of cultural property or heritage can be seen as protecting a human right. - Cultural identity in its collective dimension may contribute to constituting a group and hence be one factor giving rise to the right to self-determination.

3. Cultural Heritage may, however, also be in conflict with, and even threaten, human rights. - A concept of cultural heritage may be used as a justification for restricting human rights by those who violate human rights. - Human rights might require the eradication of ‘outdated’ cultural practices and heritage.3 - This clash leads to the core of the arguments around the universal v. relative nature of human rights themselves.

4. There may be a parallel on the level of norm enforcement: individual criminal liability may be incurred for the violation of both human rights and cultural property or heritage. The intentional destruction of the Buddhas of Bamiyan or the shelling of the Old Town of Dubrovnik or the looting of the Bagdhad Museum may lead to such individual responsibility.

Changing Terminology, Changing Concepts: ‘Cultural Property’ and ‘Cultural Heritage’ in International Law

Obligations of States to Protect Cultural Property in Armed Conflicts

International legal documents reveal a remarkable expansion of the notion of protection of ‘culture’. Characteristically, long before the genesis of international human rights in the wake of the atrocities of the Second World War,4 ‘cultural property’ (beni culturali) was protected in international humanitarian law of armed conflict,5 under customary law,6 and through the Hague Conventions of 1899/1907.7 It is in this tradition8 that the 1954 Hague Convention on the Protection of Cultural Property in Armed Conflict9 defines its object: Article 1. Definition of cultural property

‘For the purposes of the present Convention, the term “cultural property” shall cover, irrespective of origin or ownership: (a) movable or immovable property of great importance to the cultural heritage of every people, such as monuments of architecture, art or history, whether religious or secular; archaeological sites; groups of buildings which, as a whole, are of historical or artistic interest; works of art; manuscripts, books and other objects of artistic, historical or archaeological interest; as well as scientific collections and important collections of books or archives or of reproductions of the property defined above;10 (b) buildings whose main and effective purpose is to preserve or exhibit the movable cultural property defined in sub-paragraph (a) such as museums, large libraries and depositories of archives, and refuges intended to shelter, in the event of armed conflict, the movable cultural property defined in sub-paragraph (a); (c) centers containing a large amount of cultural property as defined in sub-paragraphs (a) and (b), to be known as “centers containing monuments”.’

This definition is explicitly or impliedly characterised by - referring to high culture; - comprising only tangible objects that embody culture, such as archaeological, ethnographical, historical works, objects of art and architecture;11 and - giving the reason for the protection as being the universal value of the cultural property. To this extent, the definition resembles closely the one in the 1972 World Cultural and Natural Heritage Convention.12 It follows from this that states are under an objective obligation to protect such property in armed conflict (or in peace time because armed conflict is a possibility). Although the purpose of the rules is also to protect the ‘cultural heritage of every people’, i.e. for the sake of the people, it is not doing so through granting subjective individual rights.13 Sadly, recent conflicts in Yugoslavia (Dubrovnik), Afghanistan (Bamiyan Buddhas)14 and Iraq (National Museum in Baghdad), to name but a few prominent examples, show that this classic aspect of the protection of culture through the protection of cultural property remains utterly relevant, so there is a need to strengthen the protection, as attempted by the 1999 Protocol to the Hague Convention of 1954. Although the thrust of these norms is to protect cultural values per se and objectively, we can already establish a link to group identities where cultural property of prime importance for a group is destroyed with the intent to destroy the identity of this group, for example as a tool for ‘ethnic cleansing’ or genocide.

Expansion of the Notion: From Cultural Property to Cultural Heritage

It is in this direction of cultural identity that the notion of cultural heritage (patrimonio culturale) has expanded and partly replaced the term ‘cultural property’, which reflects a narrower western concept of cultural property, with connotations of property as such.16 The link is a growing body of human rights law in the context of both culture and cultural identity. This trend is also a reaction to the problems faced especially by indigenous populations17 to protect their intangible cultural property, such as motifs, songs, prayers, ceremonies, music, legends and folkore18 which may be threatened just as much as tangible cultural property. This qualitative change happened some time in the 1990s. Besides the protection of cultural property mentioned above, a new dimension of the protection of culture has developed19, that of cultural heritage.

The 2003 Convention for the Safeguarding of the Intangible Cultural Heritage (CICH) reflects this concept.

‘1. The “intangible cultural heritage” means the practices, representations, expressions, knowledge, skills – as well as the instruments, objects, artefacts and cultural spaces associated therewith – that communities, groups and, in some cases, individuals recognize as part of their cultural heritage. This intangible cultural heritage, transmitted from generation to generation, is constantly recreated by communities and groups in response to their environment, their interaction with nature and their history, and provides them with a sense of identity and continuity, thus promoting respect for cultural diversity and human creativity. For the purposes of this Convention, consideration will be given solely to such intangible cultural heritage as is compatible with existing international human rights instruments, as well as with the requirements of mutual respect among communities, groups and individuals, and of sustainable development.’

The Convention gives the following examples of intangible cultural heritage:

‘2. The “intangible cultural heritage”, as defined in paragraph 1 above, is manifested inter alia in the following domains:
  • (a) oral traditions and expressions, including language as a vehicle of the intangible cultural heritage;
  • (b) performing arts;
  • (c) social practices, rituals and festive events;
  • (d) knowledge and practices concerning nature and the universe;
  • (e) traditional craftsmanship.’

In contrast to the notion of cultural property, ‘cultural heritage’ focuses on an established ‘cultural identity’. This means that the concept goes beyond protecting tangible objects of objectively or universally recognised ‘high culture’. It also comprises individually or collectively subjectively relevant, intangible forms of cultural expressions which need not be ‘high culture’ provided that they are recognised by a community or individual ‘as part of their cultural heritage’. This feature makes the concept more subjective, recognising that it is not so much by ‘material improvement, but by customs and traditions, historical identification and religion’ that identity is established. Language and skills may be added, and the relevance of cultural to individual identity and hence to human dignity.22 Artistic or creative expressions may be both the origin and expression of cultural identity. Therefore, they are to be respected, and an alien culture is not to be superimposed. Although cultural heritage is a dynamic concept, there is also a historical element to it (‘transmitted from generation to generation’). This resounds in the term’s common root with the ‘inheritance’ of universal cultural property.23 Therefore, not only the Byzantine church of San Vitale in Ravenna, which concomitantly is cultural property, is part of cultural heritage, but also the A Tenore song from Sardegna.24 This was recently included on the UNESCO Masterpieces list of Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity25, which closely resembles the World Heritage list. In addition, there is an element of actual or perceived threat to cultural heritage inherent in the concept, hence requiring preservation. This is revealed in an exemplary manner by the explanation why the A Tenore song as a form of cultural expression needs protection in the UNESCO Masterpieces list:

‘Like many oral traditions, the A Tenore song is especially vulnerable to changes in the socioeconomic structure, such as the decline of the pastoral culture and the increase of tourism in Sardinia. The diversity of forms and repertoire is slowly decreasing. There is a tendency to perform on stage in front of (tourist) audiences, which affects the originally very intimate way this music was performed. Furthermore, many young people emigrate from the rural areas, making the transmission of the A Tenore songs within the villages increasingly difficult.’26

A further dimension, which is not yet clearly reflected in the 2003 Intangible Cultural Heritage Convention, was added by the 2005 Convention on the Protection and Promotion of the Diversity of Cultural Expressions (CCDE): the protection of the plurality of culture and cultural diversity per se. This opens up new scope for positive legal obligations – obligations to protect and possibly to promote cultural heritages. It is not only for the sake of protecting existing and specific cultural identities any more that cultural heritage is protected, but – not unlike the protection of biodiversity – cultural diversity is recognized as a common heritage of humanity. It may be debated whether the protection of biodiversity and cultural diversity are sufficiently similar to share the same rationale.27 This may at least be questioned with regard to the somewhat utilitarian rationale of preserving genetic diversity in a biodiversity context. On a wider scale, where diversity may be considered a value and aim in itself, there may be more in common. However, the recognition of the importance of cultural diversity per se is legally significant because it broadens the scope of legal obligations that can be derived from the concept of cultural heritage towards positive obligations: not only well established cultural expressions may claim to be protected from state interference. A threatened expression of cultural heritage may actively require states to safeguard it. If a threatened heritage ceases to exist, it is not relevant for a cultural identity any more and would lose its protection as cultural heritage. However, with an increasing recognition that not only an individually identity-relevant cultural heritage deserves protection but also the very plurality of such heritage, active steps might be required to maintain cultural diversity. This would result in obligations even to maintain and develop new cultural heritage – a new type of obligation that might be seen as counterbalancing the fact that some cultural heritages also become unacceptable in view of the development of human rights.28 In this sense, the preamble of the 2005 Convention on the Protection and Promotion of the Diversity of Cultural Expressions stresses both the openness by form and substance of cultural expressions and content and that cultural diversity per se is ‘a common heritage of humanity’:

‘Affirming that cultural diversity is a defining characteristic of humanity, Conscious that cultural diversity forms a common heritage of humanity and should be cherished and preserved for the benefit of all,…’29

There is not only a shift from highbrow tangible cultural property of universal value to a more pluralist conception of culture by content and form and geographic perception. There is a further shift in what amounts to higher ranking (universal) cultural heritage of mankind.30 Whereas traditionally one could make out absolute or universal cultural values, today it is at least also stressed in the 2005 Convention on the Protection and Promotion of the Diversity of Cultural Expressions that the very plurality of cultural ‘heritages’ amounts to a common heritage of mankind. This means a shift from a substantive identity-based to a formal diversity-based definition, and might be seen as a reaction and counterpoint to economic globalisation with its actual or perceived loss of diversity.31 This was, for example, also the conclusion of the UN Commission on Human Rights’ (Subcommission on Protection of Minorities) Special Rapporteur, who formulated Principles and Guidelines for the Protection of the Heritage of Indigenous People:

‘The effective protection of the heritage of the indigenous peoples of the world benefits all humanity. Cultural diversity is essential to the adaptability and creativity of the human species as a whole.’32

Calling the plurality of cultural heritage a common heritage of mankind places the concept at least from a terminological perspective into the context of the idea of a common heritage of mankind developed for physical spaces. It has so far been used for the deep seabed, the Antarctic and outer space, which are characterised by being free from individual states’ sovereign rights, not being subject to appropriation, being managed and their benefits exploited in a shared and equitable manner, and by being supposed to be used only for peaceful purposes. The already established concept of common heritage, therefore, does not fit cultural heritage very well in so far as the common heritage has a physical connotation of space. The recognition of a universal value of the plurality of cultural heritage that is inherent in calling it a ‘global commons’ seems to be more akin to the value-oriented concepts of jus cogens or erga omnes obligations or the idea of ‘humanity’ protected by criminalising on the international level crimes against humanity.33 In its other respects, the 2005 Convention confirms the definition of the 2003 Intangible Cultural Heritage Convention:34 Culture here is pluralistic, not confined to highbrow culture. The concept is broadened from cultural property as an absolute term to cultural heritage, which may be more local, more subjective, more relative and in whatever, tangible or intangible form, but there must be an artistic dimension and one of creativity. In its orientation towards identity and its historic dimension (‘passed on’), cultural heritage differs from another one to be found in the context of the protection of culture and cultural rights: that of cultural life. The concept of cultural expression can be seen as the mediator between cultural life and cultural heritage. On the one hand, it is oriented towards identity and by has a historic dimension, shared by cultural heritage; on the other hand, the Convention stresses the plurality of cultural expression which brings it close to the concept or cultural life.

Cultural Life

Looking at ‘culture’ in a legal framework, a further concept and meaning can be distinguished: cultural life (vita culturale) in an anthropological meaning35, describes a community’s way of life in a pluralist sense. It is even broader than the concept of cultural heritage and comprises wider forms of creative or expressive activity.

‘The totality of the knowledge and practices, both intellectual and material, of each of the particular groups of a society, and – at a certain level – of a society itself as a whole. From food to dress, from household techniques to industrial techniques, from forms of politeness to mass media, from work rhythm to the learning of family rules, all human practices, all invented and manufactured materials are concerned and constitute, in their relationships and their totality, “culture”’.36

‘Cultural life’ is reflected in the human right to participate freely in cultural life in Art. 27 of the 1948 Universal Declaration of Human Rights and Art. 15 of the 1966 International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR)37 and has been elaborated upon in the 1976 UNESCO Recommendation on Participation in Cultural Life. The Recommendation especially broadens the concept of culture

‘to include all forms of creativity and expression of groups or individuals, both in their ways of life and in their artistic activities’.38

The preamble states the reason for this:

‘culture is not merely an accumulation of works and knowledge which an elite produces, collects and conserves in order to place it within reach of all; or that a people rich in its past and its heritage offers to others as a model which their own history has failed to provide for them; that culture is not limited to access to works of art and the humanities, but is at one and the same time the acquisition of knowledge, the demand for a way of life and the need to communicate.’39

With a broad understanding of ‘cultural heritage’, there is a great deal of overlap between cultural life and cultural heritage. In contrast to the concept of cultural heritage, on the one hand, there appears to be no necessary (but a possible) link to expressions of human creativity with the idea of cultural life; other forms of expression, such as sports, games and hobbies, could be included.40 On the other hand even a certain sport, say cricket, may be said to express a creative idea of a group in the wider sense and thus participate in the cultural heritage of a group – or nation. Yet it should be possible to distinguish – at least in theory – between mere ‘cultural life’ or the human right to participate in cultural life and ‘cultural heritage’ along the lines of cultural identity, because there is at least not necessarily a dimension of cultural identity in all forms of cultural life.41 In practice, it will be very difficult to distinguish whether practices are relevant for cultural identity or not. Even where they are not, cultural heritage and cultural life and cultural rights on an individual level share one of their rationales – to allow for the free development of the individual in the context of his or her community. Wherever the distinction may be drawn, the right to participate in cultural life is important for the concept of cultural heritage as a right to develop a cultural identity and hence new forms of cultural heritage.42 While potentially all forms of expression can make this transition to become identity-relevant, many of them will not.

Cultural Rights

Cultural rights (diritti culturali) as laid down as a second generation of human rights, most prominently in the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights of 1966, are individual human rights in the sphere of culture to engage in, participate in and to oblige the state ‘to the maximum of its available resources’43 to maintain and promote an infrastructure of culture, such as education. They are likely to be more clearly defined rights that also incidentally protect cultural heritage. They are thus related to individual identity and the development of a personality, linking cultural rights and human dignity. Although they are conceptualised as individual rights, they may have a collective connotation where the exercise of a cultural right requires communication, similar to the right of assembly. In contrast to the concept of a cultural heritage, they do not make reference to cultural identity, especially not that of a group. They can, however, function as the precondition for identity-based rights and as a starting point for the evolution of new expressions of cultural heritage.

Holders of a Right to ‘Cultural Heritage’ – A Subjective Right?

Although the CICH 2003 and CCDE 2005 name as their objective the protection of intangible cultural heritage and the diversity of cultural expression, the obligations laid down are technically speaking obligations on states to do so via their national cultural policies, and to some extent through international cooperation. The instruments are therefore, on the face of it, mere interstate obligations and do not confer a right to cultural heritage on individuals or groups, although these are intended to be the beneficiaries. In this respect they are similar to the humanitarian law of armed conflict, as reflected, for example, in the 1954 Hague Convention and its protocols and the early minorities protection treaties which impose duties on states but do not confer individual rights. A further prominent example is the Genocide Convention of 1948 with its obligations on states to prevent and to punish acts of genocide.44

However, the first step in the genesis of a subjective right is to remove subject matters, which regularly reflect international values that have become recognised, from the whim of state sovereignty.45 The additional qualitative development of a subjective right may or may not follow subsequently. This analysis is based on the positivist premise that an individual is subject (rather than an object or mere beneficiary) of international law where he or she is vested with legal rights (rather than ‘natural rights’) that can be enforced somehow. The evolution towards a more relative and subjective value of cultural heritage goes hand in hand with the emergence of individuals or groups (minorities, peoples) acquiring subjective rights generally and possible rights to cultural heritage in particular because of the following factors:

  • There is an existing body of established human rights per se, and there is a close relationship between ‘cultural heritage’ and existing subjective cultural rights.
  • Cultural heritage and cultural rights are linked by their reference to ‘cultural identity’. This underlying rationale may serve as communicating tubes mutually cross-fertilising cultural rights and cultural heritage norms.
  • Cultural identity is part of an individual’s personality and therefore, has a close relationship with, and is an element of an emerging right to human dignity;46 the latter becomes more and more a separate human right also under international law, for example via Art. 3 of the European Convention on Human Rights.47 The partly shared rationale of cultural heritage protection and human rights might lead to an alignment of cultural heritage with human rights, which might help raising cultural heritage to the level of subjective rights to cultural heritage.
  • Last but not least: cultural heritage, through its constituting factor of being relevant to cultural identity, is closely linked to the right of peoples to selfdetermination. Even if the principle of self-determination itself is loaded with uncertainties about the holder and the scope of the right, cultural identity can be described as one out of several factors that might define a group that may be entitled to the right to self-determination.

The Added Value of a Separate Individual or Group Right to Cultural Heritage

A number of specialised individual rights, such as the freedom to have and to exercise one’s religion, freedom of expression, privacy as the right freely to develop one’s personality, freedom of assembly and association, protect aspects of what is comprised by cultural heritage. Cultural heritage will have an independent meaning in law only if it denotes a content beyond being a mere umbrella term for individual rights.48 Again one can point to the element of cultural identity that adds to most of the rights mentioned a more subjective, intangible element linking the individual’s identity with that of other individuals or that of a community. The protection of cultural identity as a more specific aspect or the right to self-determination could promote this collective right, giving it more specific content and hence possibly increase its degree of adherence and enforcement. Consequently, duties incumbent on states to promote cultural heritage would be more ‘holistic’ than duties to promote narrower, specific human rights.

A further advantage of a separate individual or collective right may be that the individual or non-state actor gains significance in enforcing international law, either on the international plane or via available national mechanisms. Even though weak, an enforcement interest may add to the enforcement of norms which otherwise remain all too abstract public interests.

An Individual Right to Cultural Heritage?

One could try to compile a list of rights drawn from international human rights law that incidentally or directly protects cultural heritage. These individual human rights, which also protect aspects of cultural heritage and are specific expressions of human dignity, might provide an argument for an emerging individual right to cultural heritage. Among the more obvious rights that have a more direct impact on culture would be the protection of privacy49 (private life), family, home, correspondence (Art. 17 of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights of 1966, ICCPR), freedom of thought, conscience and religion (‘to have or to adopt’, Art. 18 ICCPR), freedom of opinion and expression (Art. 19 ICCPR), the right to peaceful assembly and freedom of association (Art 21, 22 ICCPR) and the protection of the family, freedom to marry (and not to marry – ‘free and full consent’, Art. 23 ICCPR). The right to education (Art. 13 ICESCR) and the ‘right of everyone to take part in cultural life’ (Art. 15 ICESCR and Art. XIII of the American Declaration or the Rights and Duties of Man, Art. 17(2) of the African Charter on Human and Peoples’ Rights (1981), Art. 27(1) of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights.

This is not the place to enter into a debate about whether a right to cultural heritage may be an individual human right or a right of groups or peoples. It is tempting to leave the question open with O’Keefe, as it could be either, according to context.50 The case for an individual right, separate and independent from codified specific cultural rights, seems to be weaker than for a collective right (leaving aside for the moment questions relating to collective rights as a matter of principle). Although cultural rights are individual rights and share the underlying link to human dignity and might share at least some of the rationales of the protection of cultural heritage, it seems hardly possible to derive from this proximity a subjective individual right to cultural heritage. The links seem too vague and tenuous to draw such a far-reaching conclusion of a subjective right. Also, the precise content of cultural rights and matching obligations on states are not easily established.51 One does not have to subscribe to the restrictive view that social (and cultural) rights are not fully contingent on economic resources. Even if one assumes instead that states are under an obligation to ‘ensure the satisfaction of, at the very least, minimum essential levels of each of the rights’ contained in the ICESCR 1966,52 there is still a lot – too much – uncertainty. Deriving a subjective right to a cultural heritage from the concept of a ‘minimum core obligation’53 seems to go one step too far.

Further, raising cultural heritage to such an elevated position of a subjective individual right would not be without problems for the protection of human rights generally.54 Indeed the broader the protection of culture is drawn, as reflected in a widening of the definition of culture from absolute universal cultural property to subjective and more relative or pluralist forms of intangible expressions and diversity itself, the more difficult it becomes to establish the content of such a right. This is even more so where the dimension of protection would be not just in the negative duty not to destroy or to prevent destruction but in the positive dimension of human rights, requiring positive state action to promote cultural expressions. This is reflected in Art. 17(3) of the African Charter on Human and Peoples’ Rights which refers – in the context of positive obligations – to a duty of the state, not an individual right:

‘The promotion and protection of morals and traditional values recognized by the community shall be the duty of the state’.

A Collective Right to Cultural Heritage

The case for a collective right to cultural heritage seems to be more promising and more fitting, although its ally, the right to self-determination, is itself lacking clarity and shape. A right to cultural heritage can be partly seen as a special right derived from the right to self-determination of peoples to ‘freely pursue their economic, social and cultural development’ (common Art. 1 of the ICCPR and ICESCR; Art. 22 of the African Charter) as a third generation of human rights, alongside the other derived rights to permanent sovereignty over natural resources and the right to development. Rhetoric resembles that used in the context of self-determination with regard to colonial peoples and with regard to international development. In the same vein, the fact that the right of ethnic or linguistic minorities ‘to enjoy their own culture, to profess and practise their own religion, or to use their own language’ is mentioned and protected in Art. 27 ICCPR, may be used in support of a group right. Further, the right to develop a cultural identity,laid down in Art. 1(2) of the UNESCO Declaration of the Principles of International Cultural Co-operation 1966, could be mentioned as necessarily being a collective right. Making ‘cultural identity’ part of the definition of cultural heritage seems to reflect a trend for cultural heritage protection to move towards collective and group rights.55 The hard legal provisions are those of the general provisions on self-determination of the 1966 Covenants and the African Charter. Art. 27 ICCPR is more ambiguous, and it is debated whether it is an individual or collective right, but it could arguably support the trend. But there is plenty of ‘soft law’ reflecting the tendency of cultural heritage protection to move towards a right of people as a new generation of cultural rights. The Principles of UN Special Rapporteur Daes on the Protection of the Heritage of Indigenous Peoples of 1995 explicitly state that the protection of indigenous peoples’ heritage should be based on the right to self-determination.56 Art. 1 (2) UNESCO Declaration of the Principles of International Cultural Co-operation 1966 states:

‘Each people has the right and duty to develop its culture.’

Similarly, Art. 2 of the 1976 Algiers Universal Declaration of the Rights of Peoples reads:

‘Every people has the right to the respect of its national and cultural identity.’

It thus recognises the subjective, group-identity-related core of the definition of cultural heritage. The right is formulated in the context of the right of a people to existence, which is the most basic right derived from the principle of self-determination. As a result of legal rules centred around the individual or groups and peoples, the development of ‘culture’ in international law follows the general trend towards a less state-centred international order, more similar to that of Gentili, who was criticised as not having been at the height of his time because he did not have a state-centred image of the world according to the then emerging Westphalian model.57 At least from today’s perspective he can be said to have been ahead of his time.

Problems relating to a Right to Cultural Heritage

In spite of some potential benefits of a subjective right to cultural heritage, there are distinct problems and dangers:

Legal Guarantees of Factual Circumstances?

One could question whether cultural heritage is a concept that can be regulated by law at all because it is the result of sociological, anthropological and cultural processes which cannot be guaranteed by law. A related concern might be that some of these rights, like that of cultural identity, rely on joint exercise with others. They share the fact that they become meaningless when the factual circumstances are not such as to provide a basis for the exercise of cultural rights. First, this is not a specific concern with regard to cultural heritage but affects all rights that are exercised jointly with other, be they individual or collective. While it is conceded that the emergence or even continued existence of cultural heritage cannot be ensured ultimately by law, it can be protected when having come into existence; or – as might be derived from the CCDE – diversity may be encouraged and lead to the development of new cultural heritage. In the dimension of a positive obligation, therefore, the criticism is not all-defeating. Further, it is not uncommon for human rights to protect subjective concepts: the freedom of religion would be another example.

Problems of Definitions and Conceptualisation

A different concern arises from the lack of precise legal definitions. It has to be said immediately that these cannot be resolved in this paper; they are problems of third generation rights generally.58 These problems may affect the status of the protection – as an obligation on states or a subjective right.59 The term ‘peoples’ is subject to a perennial debate around the holder of the right to self-determination. The concept of cultural identity is difficult to define as it is a strongly subjective notion. Defining ‘peoples’ in terms of cultural identities could be a theoretical solution, but it would be subject to similar concerns that are generally voiced in reaction to attempts to define peoples at a unit below the level of a population of a nation state.60 Hence attempts might give rise to circular definitions in relation to culture61 Further, the attempt to distinguish cultural heritage from cultural life reveals that broadening the notion of culture is a two-edged sword: it gives rise to the danger that the concept becomes meaningless if cultural life is equated with leisure activities and where it is unclear how far a practice shapes the identity of a community. Is Big Brother shaping the identity of modern British (or Italian) culture? Is football, cricket etc.? While it may be difficult, it may still be possible to make a distinction between the freedom to act and identity-relevant cultural activities in principle.

Conflicts of Rights

More problematic are some of the consequences of expanding the protection of culture to the protection of cultural heritage through individual or collective subjective rights. Where there are rights, there are conflicting rights of others. What if human rights clash with actual or perceived cultural heritage? Who is in a capacity to define whether cultural phenomena qualify as cultural heritage or how strongly they may be weighed against other interests and, most notably, human rights? One reply could be that there is nothing special about the fact that recognising a right to a cultural heritage increases the potential for clash. This happens all the time where human rights are at issue and usually leads to balancing processes, which may be criticised in individual instances but do not seem to be a fundamentally unsuitable tool. Further, a right to cultural heritage of individuals or groups may strengthen their position against their own state which may, for example, be trying to exploit the natural resources in an area that is vital for the culture of a people. It may even be directed against a foreign state, as in the Case of the Temple of Preah Vihear62 shows which is still entangled in the ongoing border dispute between Thailand and Cambodia. The case concerned the right of access to a Khmer religious site which was under Thai control but which the International Court of Justice held to be Cambodian. However, raising cultural heritage to the level of individual rights would at the same time also reduce the influence of the state to ensure and safeguard conflicting human rights.

Two Conflicting Rights Based on Cultural Heritage?

Cultural heritage may be attributed to several peoples. Where world cultural heritage is at issue, this will be quite common. The often quoted example is that of the Parthenon frieze, renamed Elgin marbles, where British and Greek claims clash that could possibly even be construed as claims on the level of cultural heritage on both sides. Leaving aside the problems raised by colonialism and unlawful acquisition of objects, a history as a collecting (and preserving) nation could become part of the cultural heritage of a nation, so that indeed two claims on the level of cultural heritage could clash. Again, this is an issue of balancing – at the same level, possibly even that of cultural heritage on both sides – which cannot be dealt with in detail here.63 One option applied in the context of ownership is the Canadian rule of 35 years of possession;64 another one could allocate decisive weight to the legitimacy of the sale and purchase, issues that were recently also illustrated by the Gustav Klimt paintings in Austria and the prototypical expressionist painting by Ernst-Ludwig Kirchner street scene in a Berlin collection.65 But this takes us into the private law side of the restitution of art that cannot be pursued in greater detail here. There may even be conflicting and mutually exclusive ways of protecting cultural heritage, as shown by the example of excavations to preserve cultural heritage which would have to happen in places of worship of indigenous population.66

Conflicts between Cultural Heritage and Human Rights

The potential clash between human rights and cultural heritage becomes very obvious where there are deeply ingrained practices that fulfil the definition of cultural heritage but at the same time violate human rights. Examples are female genital mutilation, severe dress codes, forced prostitution, ‘witch’ hunting, honour killings, forced marriages or other forms of severe discrimination against women in society. Cultural heritage protection (re)inserts cultural relativism into human rights law where a fragile consensus over the universality of most basic human rights seems to have become accepted. In the extreme this might erode the very concept of universal human rights being outside the domaine resérvé of states. The dilemma is well summarised by the report of the UN Human Rights Commission’s Special Rapporteur on violence against women on Cultural Practices in the Family that are Violent towards Women, Radhika Coomaraswamy:

‘By arguing that custom, tradition and religion cannot be invoked by State parties to defend violence against women in the family, international standards reject the cultural relativist argument that cultural practices that are violent towards women in the family should be shielded from international scrutiny.’67
‘The universal standards of human rights are often denied full operation when it comes to the rights of women. Throughout the world, there are practices in the family that are violent towards women and harmful to their health. Young girls are circumcised, live under severe dress codes, are given in prostitution, denied property rights and killed for the sake of honour in the family. This report documents a number of cultural practices which violate women’s human rights to bodily integrity and to expression, as well as undermining essential values of equality and dignity. These practices and many others constitute a form of domestic violence but have avoided national and international scrutiny because they are seen as cultural practices that deserve tolerance and respect.’
‘Cultural relativism is often used as an excuse to permit inhumane and discriminatory practices against women in the community, despite clear provisions in many human rights instruments, including the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women, in accordance with which States parties shall take all appropriate measures to modify the social and cultural patterns of conduct of men and women, with a view to achieving the elimination of prejudices and customary and all other practices which are based on the idea of the inferiority or the superiority of either of the sexes or on stereotyped roles for men and women (art.5).’68
‘Nevertheless, many of the practices enumerated in the next section are unconscionable and challenge the very concept of universal human rights. Many of them involve “severe pain and suffering” and may be considered “torture like” in their manifestation. Others such as property and marital rights are inherently unequal and blatantly challenge the international imperatives towards equality. The right to be free from torture is considered by many scholars to be jus cogens, a norm of international law that cannot be derogated from by nation States. So fundamental is the right to be free from torture that, along with the right to be free from genocide, it is seen as a norm that binds all nation States, whether or not they have signed any international convention or document. Therefore those cultural practices that involve “severe pain and suffering” for the woman or the girl child, those that do not respect the physical integrity of the female body, must receive maximum international scrutiny and agitation. It is imperative that practices such as female genital mutilation, honour killings, Sati or any other form of cultural practice that brutalizes the female body receive international attention, and international leverage should be used to ensure that these practices are curtailed and eliminated as quickly as possible.’69

The 2005 Cultural Diversity of Expression Convention recognizes the danger of relativism and strikes an a priori balance in favour of (other) human rights by stating clearly that no derogation from human rights can be justified by recourse to cultural heritage.

‘Article 2 – Guiding principles
1. Principle of respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms Cultural diversity can be protected and promoted only if human rights and fundamental freedoms, such as freedom of expression, information and communication, as well as the ability of individuals to choose cultural expressions, are guaranteed. No one may invoke the provisions of this Convention in order to infringe human rights and fundamental freedoms as enshrined in the Universal Declaration of Human Rights or guaranteed by international law, or to limit the scope thereof.’70

The CCDE refers to all and any human right under international law. The theme and sometimes uneasy fit of human rights and ‘culture’ is also taken up by human rights treaties, such as the Convention on the Elimination of all Forms of Discrimination against Women (CEDAW) of 1979 whose Art. 2 lit. f) and Art. 5 lit. a) impose a clear obligation on states

‘to take all appropriate measures, including legislation, to modify or abolish existing laws, regulations, customs and practices which constitute discrimination against women;’71

and

‘to modify the social and cultural patterns of conduct of men and women, with a view to achieving the elimination of prejudices and customary and all other practices which are based on the idea of the inferiority or superiority of either of the sexes or on stereotyped roles for men and women.’

Also, human rights modify the protection of cultural heritage to the extent that members of a community who would like to opt out of being part of the preservation and continuation of the cultural heritage, must be allowed to do so. Some sort of balancing might be permissible here. In contrast, affirmative action to compensate for past collective injustices is limited by the human rights violations created by new individual injustices. However, how legal systems strike this balance, varies. This also raises the related question how far states have a discretion (or margin of appreciation) in the interpretation of international human rights which concerns not only the overcoming of human rights violations but the very definition of the content of human rights. The practice of arranged and forced marriages, both of which might be seen as reflecting cultural heritage, exemplifies how thin the line is between accepted cultural practices and those considered to violate human rights. In the case of forced marriages, the UN Special Rapporteur considers cultural heritage aspects as secondary, differentiating from ‘arranged marriages, which operate successfully within many communities.’72 The quoted Article 2 CCDE suggests that it is not merely a result of normative hierarchy that other human rights (expression, communication) deserve priority. The priority seems to apply beyond ius cogens norms, such as the prohibition of torture and inhuman and degrading treatment and the like. While the result is welcome, it does not seem to be immediately obvious how this a priori balance can be construed in law if subjective rights conflict which are presumably of equal strength. If neither of them is a ius cogens norm, the decision can be reached only by a balancing process in each individual case. If the protection of cultural heritage is only a duty on the state (as opposed to an individual right), such a priori balancing is more easily reconcilable with general principles of human rights law.

Conclusions

It has been shown that, with the creation and evolution of the concept of cultural heritage, a trend can be sketched out. This trend is characterised by a move away from a universal, absolute and objective conception of culture to a more subjective, pluralist and to a certain extent more relative concept. This leads on the one hand to a wider protection of ‘culture’. On the other hand it also opens up new terrain for clashes with human rights. As long as this trend to increase the protection of cultural heritage and to promote plurality and diversity, and possibly new cultural heritage, occurs within a framework of human rights, it cannot be subject to infinite relativism. International law here itself becomes an input factor into the development of cultural heritage. It provides the impetus for change of a dynamic concept of cultural heritage where cultural heritage is found to be violating fundamental human rights.73 This also becomes obvious in the recommendations of the UN Special Rapporteur Radhika Coomaraswamy:

‘These ideologies and structures emerged in a different era but continue to be harmful to women and to dominate public opinion and individual lifestyles, thus preventing the eradication of practices that are harmful to women. The report emphasizes State responsibility to eradicate violence in the family and documents the positive developments strategies to deal with harmful cultural practices developed by States in cooperation with women’s organizations.’74

The report then concludes with a number of recommendations aimed at obligations and efforts to eliminate cultural practices (that may be part of a cultural heritage) that violate human rights:

123.‘States should not invoke any custom, tradition or religious consideration to avoid their obligation to eradicate violence against women and the girl child in the family. 124.(…) 125.States should develop penal, civil and administrative sanctions in domestic legislation to punish violence in the family and to provide redress to women victims, even if the violence is associated with a cultural practice. The penal sanction should be strong and effective and not merely on paper. 126.States should develop national plans of action to eradicate violence in the family, particularly violence related to cultural practices through health and education programmes at the grass-roots level. 127.(…) 128.(…) 129.States should adopt all appropriate measures in the field of education to modify the social and cultural patterns of conduct that foster cultural practices in the family that are violent towards women. 130.States should collect data and statistics on the pervasiveness of cultural practices that are violent towards women, so as to ensure the development of appropriate strategies for their eradication. 131.States should include the measures they have taken to combat cultural practices that are violent towards women in their reports under relevant human rights instruments. 132.States should recognize the important role that women’s groups and women’s organizations play in the eradication of cultural practices that are violent towards women and give them all necessary support and encouragement.’

From a perspective de lege ferenda the positive effects of a stronger protection of cultural heritage (through, for example, an individual human right) and the negative effects of less flexibility in reconciling conflicting human rights claims with cultural heritage (because they must be balanced at the same level, unless ius cogens human rights are in play) must be taken into account. While the subjective notion inherent in cultural heritage may generally be welcomed as broadening obligations on states protecting individuals or groups and in increasing the protection of cultural identities, it may be questioned whether the small added benefit of doing so by means of creating an individual right is worth the significant challenge to established human rights from a relativist perspective. An objective obligation on states to protect, safeguard and to allow cultural heritage to develop, embedded in existing human rights, seems to be the more appropriate way to reconcile the conflicting principles of cultural heritage and human rights protection.
